## The journal is not logs

- **Logs** are unstructured prose. You grep them. Your search
  depends on your choice of words.
- **The journal** is a strictly ordered, typed, per-turn event
  stream. You *query* it: by stage, by turn, by sequence. Every
  record has a stable schema.

Every runnable chapter from chapter 2 onward has been dumping
bundles to `runs/`. This chapter teaches you what those bundles
are for.

> **Bundle safety.** Bundles are ZIP/JSON archives that EasyCat
> validates for structure and path traversal, but they are not signed
> or tamper-evident. Only open bundles you generated yourself or got
> from a source you trust. (The ones checked in under `bundles/` are
> generated by the companion scripts in this repo.)

## The three bugs

The bundles live in `bundles/*.bundle` — checked in, unlike the
gitignored `runs/*` from other chapters, because this chapter
can't work without them. They were generated by a companion
script:


Each bundle records a session with a specific, planted failure.
Your job is to find the failure using only the journal.

## Investigation 1 — guided

Open `bug_01_empty_final.bundle`. Work the evidence:

1. `turn.started` at t_ms=1,000,000. User is expected to speak.
2. `stt.final` at t_ms=1,001,100. Look at `data["text"]`.

What do you see? An **empty string**. The turn committed a final
but there was no transcript.

Now look for the agent:

```bash
... --stage agent
```

Nothing. The agent stage never ran.

Write down your hypothesis — *why did STT commit an empty final?*
— in a single sentence before opening `solutions.md`.

## Investigation 2 — semi-guided

Open `bug_02_tts_stutter.bundle`. The user hears stuttering — fine,
gap, fine — between sentences. Hypotheses to walk:

- **Sentence splitter wrong?** Look at the `stage.tts.execute`
  records. How many? Three? That's correct for a three-sentence
  reply.
- **Agent stalls?** `agent.first_token` at t_ms=2,001,400 — fast.
  No other agent records; probably fine.
- **TTS slow?** Compare `elapsed_ms` on each `stage.tts.execute`.
  One is 5× the others. **Why?** The records between them —
  `ws.reconnect.*` — tell you.

```bash
uv run python docs/teaching/11-journal/investigate.py \
    docs/teaching/11-journal/bundles/bug_02_tts_stutter.bundle --stage tts
```

## Investigation 3 — unguided

Open `bug_03_ghost_interruption.bundle`. The bot *thinks* it was
interrupted but the user never spoke. Find the journal evidence
for:

- Which record told the coordinator a barge-in happened?
- What is *missing* from around that record that would confirm
  the interruption was a real user?
- What setting in the journal explains why the bot self-triggered?

Write your hypothesis in one sentence before peeking at
`solutions.md`.

## Why the journal has to be structured

Three properties make this kind of investigation tractable:

1. **Strict ordering by sequence number.** You can say "what
   happened after sequence 14" and mean it.
2. **Typed fields.** `data["text"]` is always a string; `data["t_ms"]`
   is always a float. No regex parsing.
3. **Causal grouping by turn_id.** In multi-turn bundles, all
   records for one turn share the same id and can be isolated.

### The teaching shape vs. the production shape

Chapters 2-10 and the fixtures here emit *composite events* of the
form `stage.<name>.execute` with a single `elapsed_ms` field — one
record per stage span. This is deliberate: the query layer stays
at the surface and the reader can read the bundle top-to-bottom.

The production journal in `src/easycat/runtime/` emits **paired
records**: `stage_start` at the beginning of a span, a matching
`stage_complete` at the end, with artifact refs and error blobs
in between. You reconstruct a span by matching start/complete on
a span correlation id carried in the record `data`. Paired records
buy you partial-span visibility on crashes and concurrent-span
overlap that single events can't express. Read the production
records after you've internalised the teaching ones.

Also be aware: `turn.state_changed` and `ws.reconnect.*` records
in these bundles are **representative synthetic records** — the
production pipeline emits similar signals, but chapters 2-10's
own scripts do not write them. They are here so the fixtures
tell a coherent story without running the whole framework.

Pick any bundle from your `runs/` directories — chapter 9 is

Can a classmate (or your future self) find it?
